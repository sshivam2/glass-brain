Femoral neck abnormalities

Femoral neck abnormalities are axial malalignments of the femoral neck involving the caput-collum-diaphyseal angle and the femoral angle of anteversion. Four common femoral version abnormalities are discussed here: coxa vara, coxa valga, femoral anteversion, and femoral retroversion. Clinical features include an abnormal gait and pain with prolonged weight-bearing. Some abnormalities may resolve spontaneously. However, severe cases require surgical correction. Common complications include secondary arthrosis and femoroacetabular impingement.

  • Definition: axial malalignments of the femoral neck involving the caput-collum-diaphyseal angle (CCD angle) and the angle of anteversion
  • Femoral neck axis: a line that equally divides the femoral neck through the center of the femoral head [1]
  • Caput-collum-diaphyseal angle (CCD angle) [1]
    • The angle formed by the main axis of the femoral neck and the longitudinal axis of the femoral shaft (coxa norma)
    • Normal angle [2]
      • ∼ 125° in adults
      • ∼ 150° in newborns
  • Femoral anteversion angle [3][4]
    • The angle formed by the longitudinal axis of the femoral neck and the transverse axis of both femoral condyles of the distal femur
    • Indicates the normal torsion of the femur
    • Normal angle
      • 8–15° in adults
      • 30–40° in newborns
  • Hilgenreiner epiphyseal angle [5]
    • The angle formed by the intersection of a line drawn from the triradiate cartilages (Hilgenreiner line) and another line drawn through the capital femoral physis
    • Used to measure the severity of femoral deformities
    • Normal angle: ∼ 25°

Femoral neck anomalies are initially diagnosed using plain x-ray.

  • Definition: a deformity of the proximal femur due to a decreased femoral neck-shaft angle (< 120°), with shortening and thickening of the femoral neck
Overview of coxa vara [5][6]
Congenital Developmental Acquired
Etiology
  • Present at birth
  • Typically occurs with other skeletal deformities or as part of a syndrome
    • Skeletal deformities
      • Spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia
      • Multiple epiphyseal dysplasia
      • Cleidocranial dysplasia
      • Proximal femoral focal deficiency
      • Fibular deficiency
      • Congenital short femur
    • Associated syndromes
      • Femoral facial syndrome
      • Camptodactyly arthropathy coxa vara pericarditis syndrome
  • Develops during the perinatal period and may progress during childhood
  • Secondary to metabolic, neoplastic, or traumatic conditions
    • Infection (e.g., septic arthritis, osteomyelitis)
    • Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease
    • Osteopetrosis
    • Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE)
    • Fibrous dysplasia
    • Paget disease of bone
    • Vitamin D-dependent rickets
    • Trauma (e.g., fracture of the femoral neck or trochanter, epiphyseal injuries)
    • Vascular injury to the femoral growth plate (e.g., avascular necrosis of the femoral head)
    • Osteomalacia
    • Osteogenesis imperfecta
Clinical features
  • Unilateral involvement (typically in the subtrochanteric area)
  • Abductor muscle weakness
  • Limb length discrepancy (shorter leg on the affected side)
  • Associated with:
    • Femoral retroversion
    • Knee valgus
  • Presents by walking age (2–3 years of age)
  • Bilateral involvement
  • Trendelenburg gait
  • Limited hip abduction and internal rotation
  • Positive Trendelenburg sign: The hip is abducted when reduced.
  • Clinical features of the underlying disorder
  • Typically, unilateral involvement
  • Limb length discrepancy
Diagnostics
  • AP and lateral x-rays
    • Reduced angle between femoral neck and shaft
    • Vertically orientated and widened physis
    • Overgrowth of the greater trochanter
    • Small fractures may be present medially.
  • MRI
    • To assess the hip joint cartilage and labrum
    • Findings: widened physis, bone marrow edema, and expanded cartilage tissue
  • AP and lateral x-rays
    • Triangular shaped ossification of the femoral neck
    • Changes in the femoral head and acetabulum
    • Widening of the physis
  • CT/MRI: presurgical planning
  • Depends on the underlying disorder (e.g., laboratory tests to diagnose rickets, vitamin D deficiency)
  • AP and lateral x-rays: reduced angle between femoral neck and shaft
Treatment
  • Expectant management
    • Hilgenreiner epiphyseal angle < 45°
    • Asymptomatic patients with Hilgenreiner epiphyseal angle of 45°–60°
  • Surgery: valgus derotation osteotomy (at the subtrochanteric or intertrochanteric level) [7]
    • Progressive or severe deformities
    • Symptomatic patients with a Hilgenreiner epiphyseal angle of 45°–60°
    • A Hilgenreiner epiphyseal angle > 60°
  • Depends on the underlying disorder
Complications
  • Persistence of abnormal gait and functional impairment
  • Hip dysplasia
  • Hip subluxation
  • Premature closure of the femoral physis
  • Pseudoarthrosis
  • Osteoarthritis

  • Definition: deformity of the femur due to an increased femoral neck-shaft angle (> 140°)
Overview of coxa valga [6][8]
Congenital Secondary
Etiology
  • Seen in newborns or toddlers
  • Possible genetic predisposition
  • Low axial load
    • Neuromuscular disorders (e.g., cerebral palsy, meningomyelocele)
    • Skeletal dysplasias (e.g., hip dysplasia, Turner syndrome)
  • Trauma leading to arrest of the growth plate
Clinical features
  • Usually asymptomatic
  • Typically bilateral
  • Groin pain due to long periods of weight-bearing
  • Limited hip abduction
  • In some cases, a positive Trendelenburg sign
  • Depend on the underlying disease
  • Typically unilateral
  • Limited hip abduction
  • Positive Trendelenburg sign
Diagnostics
  • AP x-ray of the hips: increased angle between femoral neck and shaft
Treatment
  • Spontaneous normalization can be expected once skeletal development is complete.
  • Physiotherapy
  • Persistent cases: varus derotation osteotomy (VDRO)
  • Treatment of underlying disease
  • Physiotherapy
  • Surgical correction may be considered.
Complications
  • Secondary arthrosis

  • Definition: a forward rotation of the femoral neck in relation to the femoral condyles due to an increased femoral anteversion angle
  • Epidemiology [6][9]
    • Occurs between 3–6 years of age
    • More common in girls
  • Etiology
    • Physiologic (present in up to 10% of children)
    • Congenital
      • Genetic predisposition
      • Abnormal intrauterine position of the fetus
    • Trauma (e.g., femur fracture)
  • Clinical features
    • Typically bilateral
    • Hip extension abnormalities
      • Increased internal rotation
      • Limited external rotation
    • Bowing of the legs
    • In-toeing gait
    • Hip or thigh pain (due to long periods of weight-bearing)
    • Often associated with coxa valga
  • Diagnostics
    • Usually, a clinical diagnosis based on physical examination findings
    • Imaging
      • X-ray
      • CT/MRI
  • Treatment
    • Expectant management
      • In cases of physiologic anteversion
      • Usually resolves spontaneously by 10 years of age
    • Surgery: intertrochanteric derotation osteotomy
      • Severe gait deformities
      • Symptoms that impair normal gait
      • Children 8–10 years of age with impaired gait
  • Complications: femoroacetabular impingement (an abnormal contact between the acetabulum and proximal femur during terminal hip flexion, which leads to damage of the articular cartilage and acetabular labrum) [10]

Toddlers that repeatedly or easily stumble may have femoral anteversion.

  • Definition: a backward rotation of the femoral neck in relation to the femoral condyles due to a decreased femoral anteversion angle
  • Etiology [6][9]
    • Physiologic
    • Congenital
      • Genetic predisposition
      • Abnormal intrauterine position of the fetus
    • Trauma (e.g., femur fracture)
  • Clinical features
    • Usually bilateral
    • Usually late walkers
    • Hip extension abnormalities
      • Increased external rotation
      • Limited internal rotation
    • Out-toeing gait
    • Hip or thigh pain (due to long periods of weight-bearing)
    • Flat feet
    • Often associated with tibial torsion
  • Diagnostics
    • Usually, a clinical diagnosis based on physical examination findings
    • Imaging
      • X-ray
      • CT/MRI
  • Treatment
    • Orthotics
    • Physiotherapy
    • Splinting or casting of the legs (to stretch and improve internal rotation)
    • Surgery: intertrochanteric derotation osteotomy
  • Complications [10]
    • Femoroacetabular impingement
    • Hip osteoarthritis
