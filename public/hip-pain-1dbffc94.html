Hip pain

Hip pain can be a diagnostic challenge due to its diverse etiologies and potential for significant morbidity. It can manifest acutely or gradually and may be traumatic (e.g., fractures, muscle strains, tendon injuries) or atraumatic (e.g., septic arthritis, osteoarthritis). Clinical assessment should include details of the hip pain (e.g., onset, duration, and exacerbating symptoms) and a focused examination of the hip. Most patients presenting with hip pain should be initially evaluated with x-rays of both hip and pelvis, with additional testing based on clinical suspicion. Management is based on the underlying cause.

Lower extremity osteopathy is discussed separately.

Traumatic [1][2][3][4]

  • Bone injury
    • Hip fracture
    • Acetabular fracture
    • Iliac crest apophysis avulsion
    • Ischial apophysis avulsion
    • Stress fracture (e.g., proximal femur, pelvic)
  • Muscle injury
    • Avulsion injury (e.g., of gluteal muscle, hamstring)
    • Muscle tear (e.g., of gluteal muscle, hamstring)
    • Muscle strain (e.g., of hamstring, adductors, obturator internus)
    • Obturator internus irritation or strain
    • Sports hernia
  • Tendon or ligament injury
    • Flexor tendon tear or strain
    • Proximal hamstring tendinopathy
    • Inguinal ligament strain
    • Ligamentum teres injury
  • Joint injury
    • Hip dislocation
    • Tear of the acetabular labrum
    • Osteochondritis dissecans

Atraumatic [1][2][3][4]

  • Hip joint pathology
    • Avascular necrosis
    • Femoroacetabular impingement
    • Osteoarthritis
    • Rheumatoid arthritis
    • Osteomyelitis
    • Septic arthritis
    • Chondral lesions or loose bodies
    • Ischiofemoral impingement
    • Hip joint heterotopic ossification
  • Muscle or tendon pathology
    • External snapping hip
    • Iliopsoas tendinitis or bursitis
    • Iliotibial band friction
    • Myositis ossificans
  • Malignancy
    • Gonadal tumors (e.g., ovarian cancer)
    • Sarcoma
    • Metastasis to the hip [5]
  • Referred pain
    • Intra-abdominal (e.g., psoas abscess, inguinal or femoral hernia)
    • Intrapelvic (e.g., prostatitis, endometriosis)
  • Spinal pathology
    • Lumbar spinal stenosis
    • Disc herniation
    • Disorder of lumbar facets
    • Sacroiliac joint dysfunction
  • Nerve involvement
    • Piriformis syndrome
    • Meralgia paresthetica
    • Other nerve entrapment (e.g., obturator nerve)
  • Pain syndromes
    • Greater trochanteric pain syndrome
    • Fibromyalgia
  • Other
    • Claudication
    • Inguinal lymphadenopathy
    • Pubic symphysis dysfunction
    • Metabolic bone disease (e.g., osteoporosis)

Hip pain in children [6][7][8]

  • Infectious
    • Septic arthritis
    • Osteomyelitis
    • Lyme arthritis
  • Inflammatory
    • Transient synovitis
    • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis
    • Reactive arthritis
    • IgA vasculitis
    • Rheumatic fever
  • Trauma and overuse
    • Hip fracture
    • Hip dislocation
    • Muscle strain (e.g., adductor, hip flexors, hamstrings)
    • Stress fracture (e.g., femoral neck, pubic rami) [9]
    • Apophysitis (e.g., iliac crest, ischial tuberosity, ASIS) [10]
  • Developmental
    • Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease (LCPD)
    • Developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH)
    • Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE)
  • Neoplastic
    • Osteoid osteoma
    • Osteosarcoma
    • Ewing sarcoma
  • Vascular: Osteonecrosis of the femoral head

Focused history [1][2]

  • Inciting factor or injury
  • Onset, location, alleviating or aggravating factors
  • Other localizing pain (e.g., abdominal, back)
  • Strenuous activities (e.g., sports)
  • Constitutional symptoms

Focused physical examination [1][2]

  • Examination of the hip (e.g., range of motion, palpation, strength testing, provocation tests)
  • Examination of gait (e.g., for Trendelenburg gait, antalgic gait)
  • Examination of the abdomen
  • Examination of the spine
  • Neurovascular examination as needed to evaluate for femoral artery or sciatic nerve injury
  • Pelvic examination or DRE as needed to rule out causes of referred pain

Studies are based on clinical suspicion and may include the following:

Imaging studies [2][11][12]

  • Hip and pelvis x-rays
    • For acute traumatic or chronic hip pain
    • Views
      • Standing anteroposterior and lateral views of the symptomatic hip
      • Anteroposterior view of the pelvis
  • MRI pelvis and hip without contrast: for persistent hip pain with normal x-ray findings
  • CT pelvis and hip without contrast: for patients with contraindications to MRI (timely study)
  • Ultrasound hip: for joint effusion, bursitis, and tendon abnormalities; to guide joint aspiration
  • Additional imaging (e.g., CT abdomen, spinal imaging): if extra-articular or referred pain is suspected.

Imaging may not be required for patients with acute hip pain and a clear clinical diagnosis based on history and physical examination.

Laboratory studies [4][13]

  • CBC and ESR: assessment for inflammation or infection
  • Rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP: evaluation for rheumatoid arthritis
  • Arthrocentesis and joint fluid analysis: for suspected septic arthritis

Provide immediate inpatient care to patients with fractures or infection.

Traumatic hip pain in adults

Common causes of traumatic hip pain in adults
Condition Characteristic clinical features Diagnostic findings Management
Hip fracture [1]
  • Typically occurs in older adults
  • Usually due to fall or trauma
  • Symptoms
    • Anterior hip pain
    • Inability to ambulate with the affected leg
  • Examination findings: leg externally rotated, abducted, and shortened
  • Imaging (e.g., x-ray, MRI): fracture
  • Pain management
  • Orthopedics consult for surgery
  • See “Initial management of hip fracture” and “Treatment of hip fracture.”
Labral tear [1][2]
  • Typically occurs in younger adults, athletes
  • Usually due to:
    • Traumatic or sport-related injury
    • Repetitive motion
  • Symptoms
    • Onset may be acute or insidious.
    • Sharp or dull anterior or groin pain
    • Painful clicking or catching with activity
  • Examination findings: positive FABER test and FADIR test
  • MRI hip: labral tear
  • Surgery is considered in athletes.
Sports hernia [2][14]
  • Typically occurs in athletes
  • Usually due to complex sports-related injury
  • Symptoms
    • Gradual onset
    • Diffuse, dull anterior groin pain
  • Examination findings: pain exacerbated by Valsalva maneuver, hip flexion, and/or resisted sit-up
  • MRI pelvis: detachment or tear of adductor longus or rectus abdominis
  • Conservative management (e.g., rest, analgesics)
  • Surgery in refractory cases
Adductor strain [14][15]
  • Typically occurs in athletes
  • Often due to sports-related injury
  • Symptoms
    • Onset is usually acute
    • Anterior groin pain
  • Examination findings
    • Pain with resisted adduction
    • Tenderness of adductor muscle and/or tendon
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • Ice
  • Pain management
  • Early mobilization
  • Physical therapy

Atraumatic hip pain in adults

Common causes of atraumatic hip pain in adults
Condition Characteristic clinical features Diagnostic findings Management
Osteoarthritis [1][2][13]
  • Typically occurs in older adults
  • Symptoms
    • Gradual onset of groin pain
    • Worse with activity, e.g., climbing stairs, walking up slopes
    • Antalgic gait
  • Examination findings
    • Groin pain with passive abduction or adduction
    • Abductor weakness
    • Posterior pain with squatting
    • Decreased range of motion on passive internal rotation or adduction
  • X-ray hip
    • Narrowing of joint space
    • Osteophytes
    • Subchondral sclerosis
    • Cysts
  • Conservative management (e.g., analgesics, physical therapy)
  • Surgery if indicated
  • See “Treatment of osteoarthritis of the hip and knee.”
Femoroacetabular impingement [2][16]
  • Typically occurs in young athletes
  • Symptoms
    • Gradual onset of anterolateral hip pain
    • Pain is worse when standing up from a seated position or getting in or out of a car.
  • Examination findings: positive FADIR and FABER tests
  • X-ray hip [2]
    • Pincer deformity: excessive coverage of the femoral head by the acetabulum
    • Cam deformity: bony exostosis at the femoral head-neck junction
    • Deep hip socket
    • Retroversion of acetabulum
  • Physical therapy
  • Surgery if indicated
Osteonecrosis of the femoral head [2][17]
  • Typically occurs in patients with comorbidities (e.g., sickle cell disease, HIV) or steroid use
  • Symptoms
    • Slowly progressive pain
    • Deep anterolateral hip and groin pain with weight-bearing
    • Swelling and/or limited range of motion
  • Examination findings: positive log roll maneuver
  • X-ray hip [17][18]
    • Cystic and sclerotic changes in the femoral head
    • Subchondral collapse
    • Flattening of the femoral head
    • Crescent sign: curvilinear subchondral line in the proximal femoral head
  • MRI hip [19]
    • Visualization of the interface between necrotic and viable bone
    • Bone marrow edema, joint effusion
    • May show asymptomatic osteonecrosis of the contralateral hip
  • Pain management
  • Avoidance of weight-bearing on the affected side
  • Orthopedics consult for surgery
  • See “Treatment of osteonecrosis of the femoral head.”
Greater trochanteric pain syndrome [1][2][20]
  • Typically occurs in women aged 40–60 years [21]
  • Symptoms: lateral hip pain
  • Examination findings
    • Trendelenburg gait
    • Pain with resisted abduction
    • Tenderness over greater trochanter
  • Usually clinical diagnosis
  • Conservative management (e.g., NSAIDs, physical therapy)
  • Glucocorticoid injections
  • Surgery in refractory cases
Septic arthritis [2][22][23]
  • Typically affects patients with risk factors for septic arthritis
  • Symptoms
    • Acute onset
    • Fever
    • Anterolateral joint pain
    • Restricted range of motion
  • Examination findings: pain with movement of the affected joint, guarding
  • CBC: elevated WBC
  • Elevated ESR and CRP
  • Arthrocentesis
    • WBC count > 50,000/mm3 [22]
    • Positive synovial fluid culture
    • See “Synovial fluid analysis.”
  • Targeted antimicrobial therapy for adults with septic arthritis
  • Orthopedics consult for surgery (e.g., debridement)
  • See “Treatment of septic arthritis.”
Meralgia paresthetica [2][4]
  • Typically occurs in individuals with abdominal obesity or conditions that cause increased intra-abdominal pressure (e.g., pregnancy), or those who wear tight clothing and/or belts
  • Symptoms: paresthesia, dysesthesia, and/or pain over anterior or lateral thigh
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • Removal of compressive source
  • Weight loss
  • Supportive care (e.g., NSAIDs)

Common causes of hip pain in children [6][7][8]
Disease Characteristic clinical features Distinguishing diagnostic findings Management
Transient synovitis
  • 3–8 years of age [24]
  • Sex: ♂ > ♀ (2:1)
  • URI in recent weeks
  • Typically no fever
  • Transient hip pain
  • Normal x-ray
  • Effusion on ultrasound that should improve over several days
  • Conservative therapy only (self-limiting)
  • Pain management in children
  • Rest
  • See “Transient synovitis.”
Septic arthritis
  • Any age
  • Acute onset of pain and fever
  • Fever > 38.5°C
  • Child may refuse to bear weight.
  • ↑ CRP/ESR
  • WBC > 50,000/mm3 on synovial fluid analysis
  • Effusion on ultrasound
  • Widened joint space on x-ray (but unremarkable in early stages)
  • See “Diagnosis of septic arthritis.”
  • Empiric antibiotics for children with septic arthritis
  • Therapeutic arthrocentesis
  • Urgent orthopedics consult
  • See “Acute management checklist for septic arthritis.”
SCFE
  • 10–16 years of age [25]
  • Acute on chronic dull pain with antalgic gait
  • No fever
  • Vague hip and knee pain
  • See “Clinical features of SCFE.”
  • X-ray: displacement of the femoral head
  • See “Diagnosis of SCFE.”
  • Non-weight-bearing
  • Pain management in children
  • Urgent orthopedics referral for surgical stabilization
  • See “Treatment of SCFE.”
LCPD
  • 4–10 years of age [7]
  • Insidious onset
  • No fever
  • Vague hip and knee pain (may fluctuate with activity)
  • Can cause the child to limp
  • See “Clinical features of LCPD.”
  • Early x-ray findings: often normal
  • Late x-ray findings: increased lucency of the femoral head and widened joint spaces
  • See “Diagnosis of LCPD.”
  • Conservative therapy: limited weight bearing, physical therapy, bracing
  • Surgery (e.g., femoral osteotomy)
  • See “Treatment of LCPD.”
DDH
  • Asymptomatic during infancy
  • Inability to abduct the hip, hip pain, and limp develop later if uncorrected.
  • See “Clinical features of DDH.”
  • Ultrasound between 4 weeks and 6 months and/or x-ray after 4 months are diagnostic.
  • See “Diagnosis of DDH.”
  • Abduction brace (e.g., Pavlik harness, von Rosen harness)
  • Surgical repair
  • See “Treatment of DDH.”

Pediatric hip pathologies often present as referred pain in the knee. Children or adolescents presenting with nonspecific knee pain and no findings suggestive of knee pathology require prompt assessment of the hip.
