Dyspnea

Diagnostic approach

  • ABCDE survey
  • Focused clinical evaluation
  • ABG
  • CBC
  • BMP
  • 12-lead ECG
  • Troponin
  • D-dimer
  • BNP
  • Respiratory viral PCR
  • Chest x-ray
  • POCUS (e.g., cardiac, lung, DVT)
  • Consider advanced diagnostics (e.g., TTE, CTA chest).

Prioritize rapid identification and treatment of life-threatening causes of dyspnea.

Red flag features

  • Dyspnea at rest
  • Chest pain
  • Diaphoresis
  • Cyanosis
  • Stridor
  • Increased work of breathing
  • Abnormal heart sounds (e.g., distant heart sounds, new murmur)
  • Decreased level of consciousness
  • Agitation
  • Vital sign abnormalities (e.g., hypoxia, tachycardia, hypotension)

Anticipate rapid clinical deterioration in patients with red flag features.

Management checklist

  • IV access
  • Pulse oximetry
  • Cardiac telemetry
  • Identify and treat reversible causes of dyspnea.

Life-threatening causes

  • Angioedema
  • Anaphylaxis
  • Deep neck space infections
  • Foreign body aspiration
  • Pulmonary embolism

  • COPD exacerbation
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage
  • ARDS
  • Acute coronary syndrome
  • Acute heart failure or flash pulmonary edema

Dyspnea, or shortness of breath, is a subjective feeling of breathing discomfort. It is a commonly reported symptom in acute care and outpatient settings. Causes of dyspnea include pulmonary (e.g., pneumonia, asthma exacerbation), cardiac (e.g., acute coronary syndrome, congestive heart failure), toxic-metabolic (e.g., metabolic acidosis, medication use), upper airway (e.g., epiglottitis, foreign body aspiration), psychological, and neuromuscular pathologies. On initial presentation, it is important to immediately evaluate the patient for any urgent or life-threatening causes of dyspnea using patient history, physical examination, and diagnostic testing. Once immediately life-threatening causes have been ruled out, a more detailed patient history should be obtained and further testing performed to narrow the differential diagnosis.

See also “Respiratory failure and arrest.”

Approach [1][2][3]

Assume that all dyspnea is life-threatening until proven otherwise and perform the following steps concurrently, not sequentially.

  • Perform an ABCDE survey and place the patient in a position of comfort.
  • Manage respiratory failure if present, e.g., airway management, oxygen therapy if SpO2 < 95%, respiratory support [2].
  • Begin pulse oximetry and cardiac monitoring; establish IV access.
  • Initiate treatment for life-threatening reversible causes of dyspnea.
  • Identify and treat the underlying cause.
    • Focused history and examination
    • Basic diagnostics: initial laboratory studies , CXR, ECG, POCUS
    • Advanced diagnostic testing based on initial findings

Prioritize rapid identification and treatment of critical causes of dyspnea over advanced testing to obtain a definitive diagnosis. [4]

When evaluating a patient with dyspnea, always consider infection control and the need for PPE and patient isolation.

Red flags in dyspnea

The presence of any of these red flags suggests that dyspnea is the result of a serious pathologic process.

  • Symptoms
    • Dyspnea at rest
    • Chest pain
    • Diaphoresis
  • Signs
    • Pulmonary: low SpO2, cyanosis, stridor, signs of increased work of breathing
    • Cardiac: hypotension, distant heart sounds, new murmur, pulsus paradoxus
    • Neurological: decreased level of consciousness, agitation, focal neurological deficits
  • Laboratory findings
    • Hypoxemia
    • Respiratory acidosis

Anticipate rapid clinical deterioration in patients with red flag features.

Immediately life-threatening causes of dyspnea

See also “Rapidly reversible causes of respiratory failure.”

  • Upper airway
    • Angioedema
    • Anaphylaxis
    • Deep neck space infections
    • Foreign body aspiration
  • Pulmonary
    • Pulmonary embolism
    • Asthma exacerbation or COPD exacerbation
    • Tension pneumothorax
    • Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage
    • ARDS
  • Cardiac
    • Acute coronary syndrome
    • Acute heart failure or flash pulmonary edema
    • Cardiac arrhythmias
    • Acute valvular dysfunction
    • Cardiac tamponade
  • Other
    • Stroke
    • Diabetic ketoacidosis

The severity of symptoms reported by the patient may not correlate with disease severity. Remain vigilant for life-threatening causes of dyspnea. [2]

The diagnostic evaluation of undifferentiated dyspnea aims to first rule out immediately life-threatening causes of dyspnea and then determine the etiology, guided by the pretest probability of the diagnoses under consideration.

Initial evaluation [2][5][6]

Applicable to most patients with undifferentiated dyspnea

  • Laboratory studies
    • ABG
    • Troponin
    • CBC
    • BMP
    • D-dimer
    • BNP or pro-BNP
    • Influenza and COVID-19 testing
  • Imaging
    • CXR
    • POCUS [6]
      • Focused cardiac ultrasound
      • Lung POCUS
      • DVT POCUS
  • Other: ECG

Additional evaluation

Guided by clinical assessment and pretest probability

  • Laboratory studies
    • Respiratory viral panel
    • Sputum and blood cultures
    • Serum salicylate level
    • Thyroid function tests
    • Lumbar puncture
  • Imaging
    • Echocardiogram [7]
    • CTPA or V/Q scan [8][9]
    • CT brain without IV contrast
    • CT neck with IV contrast
  • Other: peak expiratory flow
Clinical features Diagnostic findings Acute management
Pneumonia [10]
  • Fever, chills
  • Cough, dyspnea
  • Hypoxemia
  • Crackles, egophony
  • Labs: leukocytosis, ↑ ESR/CRP, ↑ procalcitonin
  • Positive sputum culture
  • CXR: consolidation, pleural effusion
  • CT chest with IV contrast: hyperdense consolidation
  • See “Acute management checklist for pneumonia.”
COPD exacerbation (AECOPD) [11][12]
  • Dyspnea, cough, purulent sputum
  • Tachypnea, hypoxemia
  • Diffuse wheezing, decreased breath sounds
  • Increased work of breathing
  • Signs of imminent respiratory arrest: confusion, absent breath sounds, bradycardia
  • ABG: ↓ pH, ↑ PaCO2, ↓ PaO2 (respiratory acidosis)
  • CXR: hyperinflated lungs; signs of pneumonia, pneumothorax, and/or pleural effusion may be present
  • POCUS: A-lines [6]
  • See “Acute management checklist for AECOPD.”
Asthma exacerbation [13]
  • Dyspnea, cough
  • Tachycardia
  • Tachypnea, hypoxemia
  • Diffuse wheezing, decreased or absent breath sounds
  • Signs of increased work of breathing
  • ABG
    • Moderate disease: ↑ pH, ↓ PaCO2, ↓ or normal PaO2 (respiratory alkalosis)
    • Severe disease: ↓ pH, ↑ PaCO2, ↓ PaO2 (respiratory acidosis) [14]
  • Peak expiratory flow: ↓ compared to predicted or personal best
  • POCUS: A-lines [6]
  • See “Acute management checklist for asthma exacerbation.”
Tension pneumothorax [15][16]
  • Severe, sharp chest pain
  • Dyspnea, hypoxemia
  • History of trauma
  • Hyperresonance, decreased breath sounds, tracheal deviation
  • Tachycardia, hypotension
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • CXR: absent lung markings, tracheal deviation, pneumomediastinum
  • See “Acute management checklist for tension pneumothorax.”
Spontaneous pneumothorax [15][17][18]
  • Sudden, sharp unilateral chest pain
  • Acute dyspnea
  • Hypoxemia
  • Hyperresonance, decreased breath sounds on affected side
  • Crepitus
  • History of lung disease or trauma
  • CXR (in inspiration): increased lucency, displaced lung markings, subcutaneous emphysema
  • POCUS: absent lung sliding, absent seashore sign
  • See “Acute management checklist for spontaneous pneumothorax.”
Pulmonary embolism [19]
  • Pleuritic chest pain
  • Acute onset dyspnea, hypoxemia
  • Cough, hemoptysis
  • Unilateral leg swelling or history of DVT
  • Hypotension, shock (if massive PE)
  • Wells criteria for pulmonary embolism
  • Elevated D-dimer
  • ↑ Troponin, BNP
  • ECG: normal sinus rhythm (most common), sinus tachycardia, signs of right ventricular strain
  • CTA chest: pulmonary artery filling defect
  • V/Q scan: perfusion-ventilation mismatch
  • TTE: right ventricle hypokinesis with normal apical movement
  • See “Acute management checklist for pulmonary embolism.”
Fat embolism [20]
  • Triad of hypoxia, neurological abnormalities, and petechiae
  • Associated with orthopedic trauma, e.g., long bone fractures
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Fat droplets in urine or sputum
  • CXR: often normal; may show patchy bilateral infiltrates
  • CT chest: ground-glass opacities, pulmonary consolidation [21]
  • Supportive care in the ICU
  • Management of ARDS
Acute chest syndrome [22][23]
  • Chest pain
  • Fever
  • Cough, shortness of breath, wheezing
  • Signs of vaso-occlusive crisis
  • Rib or sternal pain
  • Clinical diagnosis (see diagnostic criteria for acute chest syndrome)
  • ↓ Hb, leukocytosis
  • CXR: new pulmonary infiltrate
  • See “Acute management checklist for acute chest syndrome.”
ARDS [24][25]
  • Acute hypoxemic respiratory failure (within 1 week of inciting event )
  • Diffuse, bilateral crackles
  • ABG: ↓ PaO2, ↑ A-a gradient, P/F ratio < 300 mm Hg
  • CXR: bilateral patchy diffuse or homogeneous lung infiltrates
  • TTE: normal systolic function
  • See “Acute management checklist for ARDS.”

Clinical features Diagnostic findings Acute management
Acute coronary syndrome [26][27]
  • Heavy, dull, pressure/squeezing sensation
  • Substernal pain with radiation to left shoulder
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Diaphoresis, anxiety
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness, syncope
  • Pain may improve with nitroglycerin.
  • ECG: nonspecific changes, ST-segment elevation/depression, T-wave inversions, Q waves
  • Increased or normal troponin
  • TTE: hypokinesis, regional wall motion abnormalities
  • See “Acute management checklist for STEMI” and “Acute management checklist for NSTEMI/UA.”
Cardiac tamponade [28]
  • Tachypnea, dyspnea
  • Tachycardia
  • Pulsus paradoxus
  • Cardiogenic shock
  • Beck triad
  • ECG: low voltage, electrical alternans
  • CXR: enlarged cardiac silhouette
  • TTE: circumferential fluid layer, collapsible chambers , high EF, dilated IVC
    • Inspiration: Both ventricular and atrial septa move sharply to the left.
    • Expiration: Both ventricular and atrial septa move sharply to the right.
  • See “Acute management checklist for cardiac tamponade.”
Heart failure exacerbation [29][30][31][32]
  • Chest pressure
  • Cough, dyspnea
  • Hypoxemia
  • Crackles, JVD, peripheral edema
  • Chronic exertional dyspnea and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • Labs: ↑ BNP, ↑ troponin, hyponatremia
  • CXR: diffuse opacities, Kerley B lines
  • TTE: global or focal wall abnormalities, systolic and/or diastolic dysfunction, decreased LVEF
  • POCUS: multiple B-lines [6]
  • See “Acute management checklist for heart failure exacerbation.”
Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy [33][34]
  • Signs of heart failure
  • Angina
  • Presyncope or syncope
  • Arrhythmias
  • ECG signs of LVH
  • TTE: asymmetrically thickened LV wall, LV outflow obstruction
  • See “Treatment of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.”
Atrial fibrillation with RVR [35][36]
  • Dyspnea
  • Palpitations
  • Dizziness
  • Hypotension, syncope
  • ECG: irregularly irregular rhythm, absent P waves
  • CXR: pulmonary edema, left atrial enlargement
  • See “Acute management checklist for atrial fibrillation with RVR.”
Acute mitral regurgitation [37]
  • Tachycardia, hypotension, cardiogenic shock
  • Tachypnea, dyspnea
  • Weakness, dizziness, altered mental state
  • Faint systolic decrescendo murmur
  • ECG: tachycardia, nonspecific ST-wave and T-wave changes; in some cases, signs of ischemia
  • CXR: pulmonary edema
  • TTE: mitral regurgitation, normal left ventricular size , ejection fraction intact, signs of the underlying pathology (e.g., chordal rupture)
  • See “Acute management checklist for acute mitral regurgitation.”

Clinical features Diagnostic findings Acute management
Anaphylaxis [2][38][39]
  • Hives, angioedema
  • Stridor, wheezing, dyspnea
  • Hypotension
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
  • Clinical diagnosis (see “Diagnostic criteria for anaphylaxis”)
  • See “Acute management checklist for anaphylaxis.”
Angioedema [40][41]
  • Swelling of the face, upper airway, body
  • Urticaria, hypotension, and/or bronchospasm
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • ↑ Serum tryptase (in histamine-mediated forms)
  • ↓ C4 (during hereditary angioedema attacks)
  • See “Acute management checklist for angioedema.”
Foreign body aspiration [42]
  • Choking, coughing
  • Respiratory distress, cyanosis, altered mental state
  • Wheezing, stridor, absent breath sounds
  • In chronic cases : cough, recurrent pulmonary infections
  • X-ray chest: radio-opacity in the trachea or bronchi, atelectasis
  • CT chest : direct identification of the foreign body
  • Rigid or flexible bronchoscopy: visualization of the foreign body
  • See “Acute management checklist for foreign body aspiration.”
Epiglottitis [43][44]
  • Odynophagia, pain over larynx on palpation
  • Respiratory distress, hypoxia, toxic appearance
  • Dysphagia, stridor, drooling, muffled voice
  • Absence of hoarseness, cough
  • ↑ WBC, ↑ CRP
  • X-ray of lateral neck soft tissue: thumb sign (i.e., a swollen epiglottis), vallecula sign
  • Endoscopy : edematous, inflamed epiglottis
  • See “Acute management checklist for epiglottitis.”
Deep neck infection [45]
  • Fever, fatigue
  • Dysphagia, dysphonia, otalgia
  • Unilateral erythema and neck swelling/lymphadenopathy
  • Trismus, drooling, dyspnea, stridor
  • Ludwig angina: bilateral neck swelling, lingual swelling, woody/brawny cellulitis
  • ↑ WBC, ↑ CRP
  • Ultrasound: abscess, lymphadenitis, jugular thrombophlebitis
  • X-ray of the lateral neck: widening of retropharyngeal tissues
  • CT neck with contrast: decreased definition of anatomic spaces, abscess, lymphadenitis, gas collections
  • See “Acute management checklist for deep neck infection.”
Clinical features Diagnostic findings Acute management
Salicylate toxicity [46][47]
  • Tachypnea, nausea/vomiting, tinnitus
  • Lethargy, dizziness, restlessness, diaphoresis
  • Severe toxicity: coma, seizures, AKI, pulmonary edema
  • ↑ BUN and creatinine, elevated anion gap [47]
  • ABG
    • Early/mild toxicity: respiratory alkalosis
    • Moderate/severe toxicity: mixed primary respiratory alkalosis with primary high anion gap metabolic acidosis
  • ↑ Serum salicylate levels (> 40 mg/dL)
  • See “Acute management checklist for salicylate toxicity.”
Organophosphate poisoning [2][48]
  • Cholinergic crisis
  • Bronchospasm, bronchorrhea
  • Bradycardia and hypotension
  • Weakness, paralysis
  • Diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • ↓ Plasma cholinesterase activity level
  • See “Organophosphates.”
DKA [49][50]
  • Polyuria, polydipsia
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Signs of volume depletion
  • Altered mental status, coma
  • Hyperglycemia, high anion gap metabolic acidosis, ketonuria/ketonemia
  • See “Acute management checklist for hyperglycemic crises.”
Carbon monoxide poisoning [51][52]
  • Headache, dizziness, confusion, loss of consciousness
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Shortness of breath, tachypnea
  • Chest pain, tachycardia, hypotension
  • “Cherry red” skin
  • Hypoxia
  • Clinical diagnosis: history of exposure, typical symptoms, and ↑ COHb
  • CXR: usually normal
  • ABG: ↓ PaO2, ↑ Serum COHb (nonsmokers > 3–4%, smokers > 10%)
  • See “Acute management checklist for carbon monoxide toxicity.”
Anemia [53][54]
  • Postural hypotension, dizziness, tachycardia
  • Altered mental status
  • Cool and/or clammy skin
  • Pallor, signs of bleeding (e.g., melena)
  • Tachypnea
  • Normal or ↓ Hb
  • See “Acute management checklist for anemia.”

Anything that can cause metabolic acidosis (e.g., DKA, lactic acidosis, salicylate toxicity) can also cause acute dyspnea.
