Overview of psychiatric emergencies

This article gives an overview of the most common psychiatric emergencies and how to treat them.

Overview of psychiatric emergencies
Condition Risk factors/pathogenesis Clinical features Diagnostics Management
Suicidal behavior
  • Risk factors for suicidal behavior include:
    • Previous suicide attempt (most important risk factor)
    • Psychiatric conditions
      • Schizophrenia
      • Psychosis
      • Mood disorders (especially major depressive disorder and bipolar disorder)
      • Alcohol or substance use disorder
      • Eating disorders
    • Social factors (e.g., unemployment, low socioeconomic status)
    • Personal factors (male sex, possession of firearms)
  • Clinical features of the underlying condition
  • Psychiatric evaluation, involving the direct exploration of:
    • Suicidal ideation
    • Concrete intent/plans
    • Potential underlying conditions
    • Previous suicide attempts
  • Acute management
    • Imminent risk: hospitalization (if necessary, against the patient's will)
    • Elevated risk
      • Involvement of family
      • Measures to increase patient's social contacts and interaction with medical professionals
  • Long-term management
    • Treatment of underlying psychiatric disorders (e.g., with mood stabilizers, antidepressants)
    • Counseling: providing assistance in improving the patient's social circumstances, as well as development of problem-solving and coping skills
Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
  • Central D2 receptor blockade in the nigrostriatal pathway and hypothalamus after intake of:
    • Antipsychotic drugs (high-potency FGAs > SGAs)
    • Agents that affect the CNS (e.g., carbamazepine, lithium)
    • Certain antiemetics (e.g., metoclopramide, promethazine)
  • Genetic predisposition
  • Movement impairment: muscle rigidity (lead-pipe rigidity), akinesia, tremor
  • Autonomic instability
    • Hyperthermia, diaphoresis
    • Cardiovascular instability
    • Tachypnea
  • Altered mental status: reduced vigilance, stupor
  • ↑↑ Creatine kinase
  • Leukocytosis
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Myoglobinuria
  • Discontinuation
  • Pharmacotherapy
    • Dantrolene
    • Alternatives: bromocriptine, apomorphine, or amantadine
    • Benzodiazepines
Lithium toxicity
  • Elevated lithium concentrations as a result of:
    • Overdose (e.g., due to increased dosage)
    • Renal impairment
    • Low effective circulating volume (e.g., due to dehydration, loop diuretic use)
    • Concomitant medication use that increases renal absorption of lithium (e.g., NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors)
  • Gastrointestinal: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
  • Neuromuscular
    • Ataxia, slurred speech, nystagmus
    • Fasciculations, hyperreflexia
    • Coarse tremors, seizures
  • Altered mental status: delirium, confusion, somnolence
  • Acute renal failure
  • Blood work: lithium serum levels > 1.5 mEq/L
  • Discontinuation
  • Hydration with isotonic fluid
  • Severe lithium toxicity: hemodialysis, ventilatory support if required
  • Clinical diagnosis: patient history and clinical features
Malignant catatonia
  • Associated with neuropsychiatric conditions, especially schizophrenia
  • Thought to be the result of neurotransmitter dysregulation
  • Autonomic dysregulation
    • Fever, sweating
    • Cardiovascular instability, tachypnea
  • Movement impairment: rigidity
  • Altered mental status: delirium
  • Discontinuation of dopamine blocking drugs
  • Benzodiazepines (intravenous or sublingual lorazepam)
  • ECT
Serotonin syndrome
  • Ingestion of serotonergic agents that increase the activity of serotonin receptors, including:
    • Psychiatric drugs (e.g., MAOIs, SSRIs, SNRIs)
    • Other drugs (e.g., tramadol, ondansetron, linezolid)
  • Increased risk under the following conditions:
    • Concurrent use of two or more serotonergic drugs
    • Switching from one serotonergic drug to another without tapering
  • Neuromuscular excitability
    • Hypertonia, myoclonus
    • Hyperreflexia, tremor, seizure
    • Hyperthermia
  • Autonomic dysfunction
    • Diaphoresis, mydriasis
    • Cardiovascular instability
    • Gastrointestinal upset
  • Altered mental status: delirium, psychomotor agitation
  • Discontinuation
  • Supportive care: cyproheptadine, antihypertensives, fluid replacement, benzodiazepines
Delirium tremens
  • Alcohol withdrawal in individuals with chronic alcohol use
  • Altered mental status
    • Visual and tactile hallucinations
    • Impaired consciousness and disorientation, anxiety, disorganized thinking
    • Increased suggestibility
  • Psychomotor agitation
    • Fidgeting, restlessness, emotional lability
    • Reversal of the sleep-wake cycle
    • Insomnia
  • Autonomic instability: tachycardia, hypertension, nausea
  • Additional symptoms in delirium tremens:
    • Alcohol withdrawal seizures
    • Rest and intention tremor (first high frequency, then low frequency)
  • First-line: benzodiazepines
    • Shorter or intermediate-acting (e.g., oxazepam, lorazepam): Consider in patients with a slow metabolism (e.g., the elderly, those with liver failure)
    • Longer acting (e.g., chlordiazepoxide, diazepam): preferred in all other patients
  • Second-line: anticonvulsants (in case of contraindications for benzodiazepines)
Delirium
  • Pediatric, elderly (> 65 years), and hospitalized patients are particularly susceptible
  • Most commonly associated with metabolic conditions, such as electrolyte abnormalities, thyroid disturbances
  • Other precipitating factors include:
    • Infections (most commonly UTIs, pneumonia)
    • Trauma, CNS pathologies, hypoxia, acute cardiovascular disease
    • Drugs and toxins (e.g., anticholinergics, sedatives, opioids)
    • Alcohol and illicit substance intoxication/withdrawal
    • Ongoing symptoms (e.g., dehydration, urinary retention)
  • Treatment of the underlying condition (e.g., discontinuation of precipitating medication, antibiotic treatment of infection)
  • Maintain adequate hydration
  • Reduce confusion (e.g., reorientation to time, place, and person)
Acute dystonia
  • Inhibition of nigrostriatal dopaminergic pathways caused by antagonism at D2 receptors after antipsychotic intake
  • Movement impairment
    • Painful and lasting muscle spasms and stiffness
    • Torticollis
    • Oculogyric crisis
    • In severe cases: laryngospasm
  • First-line: benztropine
  • Alternatives: diphenhydramine, benzodiazepines
  • In case of laryngospasm: intubation
Tyramine-induced hypertensive crisis
  • Ingestion of tyramine-rich foods, including aged smoked/cured meats and alcoholic beverages (especially beer and red wine)
  • Tyramine stimulates the sympathetic nervous system by releasing other neurotransmitters, such as noradrenaline, from vesicles into the synaptic cleft.
  • Hypertensive crisis (see “Clinical features of hypertensive crises” for details)
  • Usually self-limiting, depending on the amount of ingested tyramine
  • Rapid blood pressure reduction is contraindicated
Tricyclic antidepressant toxicity
  • Ingestion of tricyclic antidepressants which cause various effects, including:
    • Muscarinic ACh receptor inhibition
    • Cardiac fast sodium channel inhibition
    • GABAA inhibition
  • Anticholinergic effects
    • Hyperpyrexia, flushed and dry skin, thirst, mydriasis
    • See “Anticholinergic syndrome.”
  • Cardiotoxic effects: prolonged QTc, arrhythmias
  • Neurotoxicity: seizures, coma, respiratory depression
  • Supportive treatment, e.g., securing airways, oxygenation
  • Activated charcoal
  • Sodium bicarbonate for treatment and prevention of cardiac arrhythmia: monitor with ECG
  • ECG: prolonged QTc and QRS
