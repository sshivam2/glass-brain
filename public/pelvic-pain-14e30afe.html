Pelvic pain

Pelvic pain, whether chronic or acute, presents a diagnostic challenge because of its diverse etiologies and potential for significant morbidity. Common causes are gastrointestinal (e.g., appendicitis, diverticulitis), genitourinary (e.g., urinary tract infection, renal colic), and musculoskeletal. In female patients, obstetric and gynecologic causes, such as ectopic pregnancy and ovarian torsion, require urgent attention. Testicular torsion must be ruled out in male patients presenting with testicular pain. Assessment begins with a focused history encompassing surgical, sexual, obstetric, and social history, alongside a physical examination that may include a digital rectal examination, pelvic examination, and palpation of the inguinal canal. Basic diagnostics include urinalysis, POCUS, and β-hCG for female patients of reproductive age. Additional investigations, such as CT pelvis and abdomen with IV contrast or STI workup, may be ordered based on the suspected cause. Chronic pelvic pain typically involves consultation with specialists. Initial management prioritizes stabilization of critically ill patients. Subsequent management options depend on the underlying cause and may include surgical intervention, pharmacotherapy, physical therapy, or lifestyle modifications tailored to the patient's needs and preferences.

See “Acute abdomen” for the management of other related symptoms. See also “Dyspareunia in women.”

Approach

  • Perform ABCDE survey and stabilize critically ill patients.
  • Perform a focused clinical evaluation for pelvic pain.
  • Obtain a pregnancy test for all female individuals of reproductive age.
  • Perform a targeted diagnostic workup of pelvic pain.
  • Obtain specialty consults as needed, e.g., general surgery, OB/GYN, urology.
  • Provide supportive care for acute abdominal pain as needed.
  • Initiate empiric antibiotics for intraabdominal infections, if indicated.
  • Identify and treat the underlying cause.

Red flags

Red flags in pelvic pain

  • Sudden, severe pain
  • Peritoneal signs
  • Signs of shock
  • ↑ bHCG or known pregnancy
  • Abnormal uterine bleeding
  • Significant amounts of free fluid on ultrasound

Other

  • Red flags in abdominal pain
  • Red flags in constipation
  • Red flags in diarrhea
  • Red flags for colorectal cancer

Immediately life-threatening causes

  • Acute mesenteric ischemia
  • MBO
  • Strangulated abdominal hernia
  • Pelvic fracture with hemorrhage
  • Ruptured ovarian cyst
  • Ruptured ectopic pregnancy
  • Spontaneous abortion complicated by hemorrhage or infection
  • Complicated labor (e.g., preterm labor, obstructed labor, uterine rupture)

Fertility-threatening causes [5]

Any delay in the diagnosis and treatment of the following conditions increases the risk of fertility loss.

  • Ovarian torsion
  • Tuboovarian abscess
  • Testicular torsion
  • Scrotal abscess
  • Ectopic pregnancy
  • PID

Focused history

General history for all patients

  • Associated symptoms, including:
    • Lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS)
    • Nausea, vomiting
    • Changes in bowel movement
    • Fever, chills
  • Prior intraabdominal or groin surgery
  • Sexual history

Gynecologic and obstetric history

  • Current and prior pregnancies, including ectopic
  • If pregnant: access to prenatal care
  • Method of contraception
  • Menstrual history and irregularities, including:
    • Dysmenorrhea
    • Amenorrhea
    • Abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB)
    • Postmenopausal bleeding
  • Prior gynecologic procedures
  • Use of assisted reproductive technology

Focused physical examination

The physical examination should be tailored to the suspected underlying cause and may include:

  • Complete abdominal examination
  • Palpation of the inguinal canal in supine and upright positions
  • Inspection of external genitals
  • Bimanual pelvic examination (in female patients)
  • Digital rectal examination (DRE)
  • Examination of the bony pelvis, including pelvic stability assessment

Pelvic examinations can cause discomfort, particularly in patients with chronic pelvic pain. Obtain informed consent, start with less invasive methods, and progress as tolerated while checking in frequently. [6]

Initial approach [5]

  • Perform a focused clinical evaluation.
  • Obtain basic laboratory studies.
    • CBC, BMP
    • Urinalysis with or without urine culture
    • β-hCG (serum or urine) for female individuals of reproductive age
  • Consider point of care ultrasound (POCUS), e.g.:
    • FAST to assess for intraabdominal free fluid
    • POCUS for early pregnancy to rule in intrauterine pregnancy (IUP)
    • Bowel POCUS
    • Genitourinary POCUS

Additional diagnostics

Consider formal imaging and additional diagnostics depending on patient characteristics, pregnancy status, and the suspected underlying cause.

Imaging

  • Female individuals with positive β-hCG: ultrasound pelvis (transabdominal and/or transvaginal) with Doppler [7][8]
  • Male individuals, negative b-hcg, or postmenopausal: Obtain further imaging based on suspected underlying condition.
    • Gynecologic conditions: ultrasound pelvis (transabdominal and/or transvaginal) with Doppler [7][8]
    • Intraabdominal conditions [9][10]
      • Consider CT abdomen and pelvis with IV contrast.
      • See also “Diagnostics for acute abdomen.”
    • Musculoskeletal and traumatic conditions [11]
      • CT and/or x-ray imaging of the affected region
      • See also “Diagnostics in trauma.”
    • Genitourinary conditions [12][13][14]
      • Consider CT without contrast or ultrasound of the bladder and kidneys.
      • Scrotal pain: ultrasound with Doppler

Additional laboratory studies for acute pain

  • STI testing for suspected infectious etiologies (e.g., PID, epididymitis)
  • Coagulation studies for abnormal bleeding
  • Type and screen for significant hemorrhage, including antepartum hemorrhage
  • Emergency preoperative diagnostics if surgical treatment is anticipated

Diagnostics for chronic pain [4][15]

  • Obtain a thorough history and physical examination.
  • Consult relevant specialties (e.g., OB/GYN, urology, general surgery) to determine appropriate diagnostics.

Gynecologic causes

Common gynecologic causes of pelvic pain
Characteristic clinical features Diagnostic findings Management
Ovarian cyst rupture [16][17]
  • Sudden onset of unilateral pelvic pain
  • Usually after physical activity (exercise, sexual intercourse)
  • Minimal vaginal bleeding can occur.
  • Ultrasound: fluid in the pelvis, adnexal mass
  • Emergency laparoscopy in hemodynamically unstable patients
  • See “Treatment” in “Ruptured ovarian cyst.”
Ovarian torsion [18]
  • Sudden severe unilateral pelvic pain
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Adnexal mass may be palpable.
  • Pelvic ultrasound or TVUS with Doppler: enlarged, edematous ovaries with decreased blood flow
  • CT pelvis with IV contrast: unilateral enlarged ovary with decreased enhancement
  • Emergency exploratory laparoscopy
  • See “Management of ovarian torsion.”
Mittelschmerz [19]
  • Midcycle, sudden onset unilateral pelvic pain
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • Ultrasound may show fluid in the pelvis.
  • Reassurance
  • Analgesia
Primary dysmenorrhea [20][21]
  • Cyclical, spasmodic suprapubic pain
  • Pain may radiate to the back or thighs.
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, bloating
  • Normal pelvic examination
  • Diagnosis of exclusion
  • Symptomatic treatment, hormonal contraceptives
  • See “Treatment” in “Primary dysmenorrhea.”
Endometriosis [6][22]
  • Chronic pelvic pain
  • Dysmenorrhea
  • Dyspareunia
  • Infertility
  • Dyschezia
  • Palpable adnexal masses, reduced uterine mobility
  • TVUS
    • Endometrioma
    • Uterus size is typically normal.
  • Diagnostic laparoscopy: direct visualization of ectopic endometrial tissue
  • Symptomatic treatment, hormonal contraceptives
  • Laparoscopic ablation of endometrial implants
  • See “Management of endometriosis.”
Pelvic inflammatory disease
  • Bilateral pelvic pain
  • Cervical motion tenderness
  • Mucopurulent cervical/vaginal discharge
  • Fever
  • See “Initial evaluation for PID” for basic diagnostics.
  • Additional evaluation for PID if tuboovarian abscess is suspected
  • Antibiotic treatment
  • See “Treatment of PID.”
Cervicitis [23]
  • Purulent, blood-tinged, and/or malodorous vaginal discharge
  • Postcoital or intermenstrual bleeding
  • Dyspareunia
  • Erythematous, edematous, friable cervix
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • Microbiological studies similar to PID
  • Antibiotic treatment
  • See “Treatment of cervicitis.”
Endometritis
  • Lower abdominal/pelvic pain
  • AUB
  • Fever (if peritonitis or pelvic abscesses develop)
  • Testing for typical pathogens (e.g., chlamydia)
  • Endometrial biopsy (to evaluate chronic endometritis that is unrelated to pregnancy)
  • Mild to moderate cases (outpatient treatment)
    • One single dose of IM ceftriaxone and oral therapy with doxycycline
    • Addition of metronidazole should be considered in some cases (e.g., patients who recently underwent gynecological procedures).
  • Severe cases (inpatient treatment): clindamycin PLUS gentamicin
Uterine leiomyoma [24][25]
  • AUB, dysmenorrhea
  • Chronic back and/or pelvic pain
  • Infertility
  • Urinary tract or bowel symptoms
  • Dyspareunia
  • TVUS
    • Well-circumscribed hypoechoic solid mass
    • Calcifications and/or cystic areas
  • See “Management of uterine leiomyoma.”
Adenomyosis [26]
  • Chronic pelvic pain
  • Dysmenorrhea
  • AUB
  • Tender, enlarged uterus
  • Imaging (TVUS, MRI): asymmetric myometrial wall thickening
  • Symptomatic treatment, hormonal contraceptives, or surgical management
  • See “Treatment” in “Adenomyosis.”

Obstetric causes

Common obstetric causes of pelvic pain
Characteristic clinical features Diagnostic findings Management
Ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome [27][28]
  • History of assisted reproduction
  • Abdominal pain and distention
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Ascites
  • Oliguria or anuria
  • Dyspnea
  • Severe OHSS: signs of shock
  • Ultrasound: ascites, enlarged ovaries
  • Immediate stabilization, supportive care, and monitoring
  • See “Management” in “Ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome.”
Ruptured ectopic pregnancy [29]
  • Sudden severe pelvic pain, guarding, and tenderness
  • Vaginal bleeding or amenorrhea
  • Cervical motion tenderness, closed cervix
  • Enlarged uterus
  • Features of shock in patients with significant hemorrhage
  • ↑ β-hCG
  • TVUS
    • Free fluid within Morison pouch and/or pouch of Douglas
    • Empty uterine cavity, thickened endometrial lining
    • Adnexal mass, tubal ring sign
    • See “POCUS for early pregnancy” for the technique and “Diagnostics for ectopic pregnancy” for additional findings.
  • Emergency exploratory laparoscopy
  • See “Management of ectopic pregnancy.”
Spontaneous abortion [30][31]
  • Vaginal bleeding
  • Pain and/or cramping
  • Cervical os may be dilated.
  • Products of conception may be visible or palpable.
  • ↑ β-hCG
  • TVUS
    • Absent or abnormal sonographic signs of IUP
    • See “POCUS for early pregnancy” for the technique.
  • Expectant management, medical evacuation, or surgical evacuation
  • See “Management” in “Spontaneous abortion.”
Labor
  • Obstetric contractions
  • Rupture of membranes
  • ↑ β-hCG
  • Sonographic signs of IUP
  • See “Normal labor and delivery.”
  • See “Abnormal labor and delivery.”
Postpartum endometritis
  • Fever
  • Lower abdominal pain, uterine tenderness
  • Chills, malaise
  • Foul-smelling lochia
  • Primarily clinical diagnosis
  • Retained products of conception or signs of surgical site infection
  • Abdominal CT or ultrasound: signs of intraabdominal abscess
  • IV antibiotics
  • Curettage if there are retained products of conception
  • See “Postpartum endometritis.”

Non-sex-specific urological causes

Common urological causes of pelvic pain (non-sex specific)
Characteristic clinical features Diagnostic findings Management
Urinary tract infection/pyelonephritis [32][33]
  • Irritative LUTS (dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency)
  • Suprapubic tenderness
  • Additional features of pyelonephritis
    • High fever, chills
    • Flank pain with costovertebral angle tenderness
  • Urinalysis findings of UTI
  • Positive urine culture
  • Antibiotic treatment
  • See “Treatment of UTIs.”
  • See “Treatment of pyelonephritis.”
Renal colic [34][35]
  • Severe unilateral, colicky flank and/or pelvic pain
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Dysuria, frequency, and urgency
  • Urine dipstick and urinalysis: gross or microscopic hematuria
  • Urine microscopy: to detect crystals
  • Ultrasound abdomen and pelvis: obstructive uropathy, hyperechoic signal with acoustic shadowing
  • CT abdomen and pelvic without IV contrast: direct visualization of the calculus
  • Analgesia, medical expulsive therapy, and/or urological interventions for nephrolithiasis
  • See “Management of nephrolithiasis.”
Acute urinary retention [13]
  • Painful inability to void
  • Suprapubic pain
  • Palpable distended bladder
  • Restlessness and/or acute distress
  • Genitourinary POCUS
  • BMP: ↑ BUN and creatinine in cases of renal failure due to obstruction
  • Immediate bladder catheterization
  • See “Initial management of AUR.”
Interstitial cystitis [36]
  • Pelvic discomfort, pressure, or pain relieved by voiding the bladder
  • LUTS lasting ≥ 6 weeks
  • Diagnosis of exclusion
  • Urine cultures: sterile
  • Multimodal pain management and behavioral modification
  • See “Management of interstitial cystitis.”
Sexually transmitted infections [23]
  • Depend on specific pathogen
  • See “Overview of genital lesions.”
  • See “Diagnostics for STI.”
  • See “Treatment of STIs.”

Urological causes in male individuals

Common urological causes of pelvic pain in male individuals
Characteristic clinical features Diagnostic findings Management
Testicular torsion [37]
  • Severe pelvic and testicular pain
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Abnormally elevated position of the testis within the scrotum
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • Doppler ultrasound: twisting of the spermatic cord, reduced perfusion of the affected testicle
  • Manual testicular detorsion and exploratory surgery
  • See “Management of testicular torsion.”
Epididymitis [38]
  • Unilateral scrotal pain and swelling
  • Symptoms of UTI, including fever
  • Tenderness along the posterior testis
  • Positive Prehn sign
  • Urinalysis: pyuria
  • Doppler ultrasound: increased perfusion
  • Empiric antibiotics for epididymitis
  • See “Treatment of epididymitis.”
Acute prostatitis [39]
  • Irritative or obstructive LUTS
  • Pelvic, rectal, and/or perineal pain
  • Fever, chills, malaise
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • DRE: tender, boggy, swollen prostate
  • Urinalysis findings of UTI
  • Blood and urine cultures may be positive.
  • Empiric antibiotics for prostatitis
  • See “Treatment of prostatitis.”
Chronic pelvic pain syndrome [40]
  • Chronic pelvic, back, and/or genital pain
  • Irritative or obstructive LUTS
  • Diagnosis of exclusion
  • DRE: normal; prostate may be tender.
  • Urine cultures: typically sterile
  • Multimodal treatment
  • See “Management” in “Chronic pelvic pain syndrome.”

Common gastrointestinal causes of pelvic pain
Characteristic clinical features Diagnostic findings Management
Acute appendicitis [41][42][43]
  • RLQ, epigastric, and/or periumbilical pain (migrating abdominal pain)
  • Nausea, anorexia, vomiting
  • Clinical signs of appendicitis
  • Fever
  • See “Appendicitis risk scores.”
  • CT abdomen with IV contrast: distended appendix with periappendiceal fat stranding
  • Ultrasound abdomen: noncompressible, distended appendix; Target sign
  • Antibiotic therapy
  • Appendectomy or nonoperative management of appendicitis
  • See “Management of appendicitis.”
Diverticulitis [44][45]
  • LLQ pain
  • Change in bowel habits
  • Tender mass in LLQ
  • Fever
  • CT abdomen and pelvis with IV contrast: colonic diverticula with pericolic mesenteric fat stranding
  • Antibiotic therapy
  • Interventional and/or surgical management for complicated diverticulitis
  • See “Management of diverticulitis.”
Constipation [46]
  • < 3 bowel movements per week
  • Straining to defecate
  • Hard stools
  • Sensation of incomplete evacuation
  • Clinical diagnosis; see “Rome IV criteria for primary constipation in adults.”
  • Abdominal examination and DRE for fecal impaction
  • High-fiber diet, hydration, laxatives
  • See “Treatment of constipation.”
Inflammatory bowel disease [47][48]
  • Crohn disease
    • RLQ pain, chronic diarrhea
    • Low-grade fever, weight loss
  • Ulcerative colitis
    • Abdominal pain and cramps
    • Bloody diarrhea with mucus
    • Fecal urgency
  • Ileocolonoscopy
  • Cross-sectional imaging to identify complications (e.g., perforation or abscess)
  • Multimodal treatment
  • See “Treatment of Crohn disease.”
  • See “Treatment of ulcerative colitis.”
Obstructed or strangulated abdominal hernia [49][50][51]
  • All hernias
    • Acute pain
    • Symptoms of bowel obstruction
    • Nonreducible tender mass
  • Obturator hernia: pain and paresthesia in the inner thigh
  • Perineal hernia: mass on rectovaginal examination
  • Sciatic hernia: mass in the gluteal or intragluteal area
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • CT abdomen with IV contrast: signs of visceral ischemia, radiological signs of MBO
  • Emergent surgical repair
  • See “Management of abdominal hernias.”
Colon ischemia [52][53]
  • Sudden abdominal cramping, usually in the LLQ
  • Urgent need to defecate
  • Bloody diarrhea or rectal bleeding
  • CT abdomen with IV and oral contrast
    • Bowel wall thickening
    • Bowel edema
    • Pneumatosis intestinalis
  • Supportive treatment
  • Surgery if there are complications
  • See “Treatment” in “Ischemic colitis.”
Anorectal abscess [54]
  • Tender mass, induration and fluctuance (subcutaneous or perirectal)
  • Possibly systemic symptoms (e.g., fever)
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • MRI, CT, or endoscopic ultrasound: visualization of the abscess
  • Abscess incision and drainage
  • See “Management of anorectal abscesses.”
Inguinal or femoral hernias [55]
  • Palpable, mobile, typically nontender mass in the groin
  • Increases in size with Valsalva maneuver
  • Occult hernia: inguinodynia or inguinal discomfort
  • Clinical diagnosis
  • Ultrasound groin: visualization of the hernia sac and contents
  • Surgical repair
  • See “Surgeries for inguinal hernia.”
