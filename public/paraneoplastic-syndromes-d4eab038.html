Paraneoplastic syndromes

Paraneoplastic syndromes are a set of clinical features that are caused by either an altered immune response to a systemic malignancy or because of substances (e.g., hormones, cellular proteins) produced by tumors. They are caused neither by the local mass effect nor by metastasis of the associated cancer. Paraneoplastic syndromes present with a wide range of findings and are most commonly associated with lung, ovarian, lymphatic, and breast cancers. Small cell lung cancer is especially notorious for its numerous and distinct paraneoplastic syndromes. Some paraneoplasias are associated with many different malignancies, such as tumor-induced hypercalcemia, which is caused by parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP) produced from tumor cells. Other paraneoplasias (e.g., malignant acanthosis nigricans) are almost pathognomonic for specific malignancies and their presence demands an immediate search for the underlying tumor. A general overview of the most common paraneoplastic syndromes and their associations is presented here. Further details for individual syndromes can be found in articles of associated conditions.

Common paraneoplastic manifestations are cachexia, hyperthermia, and increased risk of thrombosis. [1]

Overview of the most common paraneoplastic syndromes [1][2]
Type Etiology Associated cancer(s) Characteristic features
Neuromuscular Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome
  • Presynaptic voltage-gated calcium channels (VGCC) (P/Q-type) autoantibodies
  • Small cell lung cancer (SCLC)
  • Proximal muscle weakness
  • Reduced or absent reflexes
  • Autonomic symptoms (e.g., dry mouth, constipation)
  • See “Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome.”
Myasthenia gravis
  • Autoantibodies against nicotinic AChRs of neuromuscular endplates
  • Thymoma
  • Fatigable weakness of skeletal muscles
    • Eye muscle weakness
    • Bulbar muscle weakness
    • Proximal limb weakness
    • Respiratory muscle weakness
  • Possibly cough, dysphagia, dyspnoea, hoarseness
  • See “Clinical features of myasthenia gravis.”
Stiff person syndrome
  • Anti-GAD or anti-amphiphysin antibodies
  • Breast cancer
  • Lung cancer
  • Lymphoma
  • Generalized increase in skeletal muscle rigidity
  • Lumbar or cervical hyperlordosis
  • Gait abnormalities
  • Episodic painful muscle spasms
Polymyositis
  • Cell-mediated cytotoxicity against unidentified skeletal muscle antigens, chiefly affecting the endomysium
  • Adenocarcinoma (most common)
  • Ovarian cancer
  • Cervix cancer
  • Lung cancer
  • Pancreas cancer
  • Stomach cancer
  • Bladder cancer
  • Proximal muscle weakness affecting both sides (progresses within weeks to months)
  • Muscle tenderness
Dermatomyositis
  • Paraneoplastic antibody-mediated vasculopathy
Neurological Paraneoplastic encephalomyelitis
  • Immune reaction against neural antigens (e.g., Hu antigens, NMDA glutamate receptors)
  • Small cell lung cancer (SCLC)
  • Anti-NMDA encephalitis: ovarian teratoma
  • Symptoms of encephalitis
    • Cognitive defects (e.g., memory deficits, speech impairment, psychiatric manifestations)
    • Seizures
    • Dyskinesias
  • Symptoms of myelitis (different patterns of limb paresis and sensory loss depending on area affected)
  • Autonomic instability
  • CSF pleocytosis
Paraneoplastic cerebellar degeneration
  • Immune reaction against neural antigens in the cerebellum (e.g., Yo antigens of Purkinje cells, Tr antigens, Hu antigens)
  • Anti-Yo antibodies: gynecological malignancies (breast, ovarian, or endometrial cancer)
  • Anti-Hu antibodies: small cell lung cancer
  • Anti-Tr antibodies: Hodgkin lymphoma
  • Ataxia, vertigo
  • Nystagmus, diplopia
  • Dysmetria, dysarthria
Opsoclonus-myoclonus syndrome
  • Cellular immune reaction against onconeural antigens → disinhibition of the fastigial nucleus of the cerebellum [3]
  • SCLC (in adults)
  • Pediatric cases of neuroblastoma
  • Ovarian cancer
  • Breast cancer
  • Rapid, multidirectional, involuntary movements of the eyes (opsoclonus ) and muscles (myoclonus)
  • Ataxia
  • Irritability, sleep disturbance
Limbic encephalitis [4]
  • Thought to be caused by an autoimmune reaction with antibodies directed against antigens richly expressed in the limbic system
  • SCLC (most common)
  • Breast cancer
  • Testicular cancer
  • Severe short-term memory impairment
  • Seizures
  • Confusion
  • Psychiatric symptoms (e.g., depression, hallucinations, anxiety)
Paraneoplastic pure sensory neuropathy
  • Immune reaction (often with anti-Hu autoantibodies) against intracellular antigens present in the primary sensory dorsal root ganglion cells
  • SCLC (most common) [5]
  • Bronchial carcinoma
  • Hodgkin lymphoma
  • Breast cancer
  • Subacute onset
  • Painful paresthesias and dysesthesias
  • Loss of proprioception
  • Progressive numbness/ weakness of feet and hands
  • Sensory gait ataxia
Endocrine Cushing syndrome
  • Neoplastic tissue produces ectopic ACTH (occasionally with CRH) → increased cortisol in the adrenal glands
  • SCLC
  • Pancreas cancer
  • CNS tumors
  • Moon facies, buffalo hump
  • Hirsutism, hyperpigmentation
  • Lethargy, depression, sleep disturbance
  • Osteopenia, osteoporosis
  • Muscle atrophy/weakness
Syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion
  • Neoplastic tissue produces ectopic ADH (endogenous) → increased free-water reabsorption and retention and hyponatremia
  • SCLC
  • CNS tumors
  • Anorexia, nausea, vomiting
  • Headache
  • Muscle cramps, muscle weakness
  • Lethargy, confusion
Hypercalcemia of malignancy
  • Humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (pseudohyperparathyroidism): PTHrP secretion by the tumor
  • Squamous cell carcinomas (lung, head, and neck)
  • Renal cancer
  • Bladder cancer
  • Breast cancer
  • Ovarian cancer
  • Nephrolithiasis, nephrocalcinosis
  • Bone pain, arthralgias, myalgias
  • Constipation, abdominal pain
  • Nausea, vomiting, anorexia
  • Ectopic vitamin D production due to 1α-hydroxylase activity in tumor cells
  • Hodgkin lymphoma
  • NHL
  • Local osteolytic hypercalcemia: osteolytic activity at sites of skeletal metastases
  • Multiple myeloma
  • Breast cancer
Mucocutaneous Malignant acanthosis nigricans
  • Ectopic transforming growth factor TGF-α and epidermal growth factor (EGF)
  • Gastric adenocarcinoma and other gastrointestinal cancers
  • Lung cancer
  • Ovarian cancer
  • Breast cancer
  • Brown to black, intertriginous and/or nuchal hyperpigmentation and dermal thickening
  • Can turn into itching, papillomatous, poorly-defined rash
  • Rapid growth and verrucous or papulous surface
  • Most commonly localized in the axilla, groin, neck, and genital/anal region
Leser-Trélat sign
  • Activation of epidermal growth factor receptors
  • Solid cancers (especially gastrointestinal adenocarcinoma)
  • Multiple, sudden-onset seborrheic keratoses
Necrolytic migratory erythema
  • Not fully understood
  • Glucagonoma
  • Multiple areas of centrifugally spreading erythema
  • Predominantly face, perineum, and lower extremities affected
  • Develop into painful and pruritic crusty patches with central areas of bronze-colored induration
  • Tend to resolve and reappear in a different location
Hypertrichosis lanuginosa acquisita
  • Response to substances or hormones released by the tumor
  • Colorectal cancer
  • Lung cancer
  • Breast cancer
  • Rapid growth of unpigmented, fine hair (lanugo hair) on face, trunk, extremities, and axillae
Skeletal Hypertrophic pulmonary osteoarthropathy (Bamberger-Marie syndrome)
  • Likely ectopic vascular endothelial GF, platelet-derived GF, and/or prostaglandin E2 → increased angiogenesis as well as fibroblast and osteoblast activity → connective-tissue matrix and bone synthesis
  • NSCLC (especially lung adenocarcinoma)
  • Features include digital clubbing, joint pain, thickening of tubular bones, periostosis, and joint effusions
Tumor-induced osteomalacia
  • Caused by the secretion of fibroblast growth factor-23 (FGF-23)
  • Slow-growing mesenchymal tumors of the limbs, nasopharynx, and/or sinuses
  • Features of hypophosphatemic rickets/osteomalacia
Hematological Polycythemia
  • EPO produced by tumor
  • Renal cell carcinoma
  • Pheochromocytoma
  • Hemangioblastoma
  • Leiomyoma
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma
  • Cerebellar hemangioma
  • Hyperviscosity syndrome
  • Plethora
  • Facial flushing
  • Pruritus
  • Dizziness, headache
  • Hypertension
Pure red cell aplasia
  • Absence of red cell precursor cells → anemia (with low reticulocyte)
  • Thymoma
  • Pallor
  • Fatigue
  • Exertional dyspnea
  • Possibly cough, dysphagia, dyspnoea, hoarseness
Good syndrome
  • ↓ B-cell counts → hypogammaglobulinemia → immunodeficiency
  • Thymoma
  • Possibly cough, dysphagia, dyspnoea, hoarseness
Trousseau syndrome (thrombophlebitis migrans)
  • Malignancy-related hypercoagulability → recurring clots that resolve and appear again elsewhere in the body (migrans)
  • Pancreatic cancer
  • Lung cancer
  • Gliomas
  • Pain, tenderness, induration, and erythema overlying the affected vein
Nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis
  • Deposition of noninfectious thrombi on the heart valves
  • Commonly associated with adenocarcinomas (e.g., pancreas cancer, colorectal cancer)
  • Fever, chills
  • General malaise, weakness, night sweats, weight loss
  • Dyspnea, cough, pleuritic chest pain
  • Arthralgias, myalgias
  • New heart murmur
Miscellaneous Neoplastic fever
  • Likely cytokine-mediated
  • Most cancers
  • Fever, general malaise, weight loss
Membranous glomerulonephritis
  • Membrane attack complex (MAC) that forms on glomerular epithelial cells
  • Lung cancer
  • Colon cancer
  • Edema (periorbital edema and(or peripheral edema)
  • Hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia
  • Hypercoagulable state (increased risk of thrombosis)
  • Increased susceptibility to infection
  • Possibly hypertension
  • Possibly frothy urine

“Rising or high hematocrit levels are paraneoplastic!” Renal cell carcinoma, Hepatocellular carcinoma, Hemangioblastoma, Leiomyoma, and Pheochromocytoma are associated with polycythemia.

Patients with OMS are described as having “dancing eyes” (opsoclonus) and “dancing feet” (myoclonus).
