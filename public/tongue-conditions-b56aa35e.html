Tongue conditions

The tongue's appearance holds a wealth of information about a patient's general health status, and specific changes (e.g., strawberry tongue) can provide valuable clues in the diagnosis of diseases such as scarlet fever, candidiasis, Kawasaki syndrome, nutritional deficiencies, and certain cancers. Accordingly, the ability to identify tongue findings may become crucial in making the right diagnosis and initiating the proper treatment early in the course of a disease.

Tongue conditions

Common tongue conditions
Features Etiology Treatment
Leukoplakia
  • Persistent white plaques that cannot be scraped off
  • Not fully understood
  • Associated with alcohol and tobacco use
  • Cryotherapy
  • Laser ablation
  • Surgical removal
Oral cavity cancer and tongue carcinoma
  • Tongue and mouth pain
  • Infiltrative or exophytic lesions: nonhealing, white or red ulcers on tongue and in oral cavity
  • Accompanied by bleeding in the mouth, halitosis, pain, and lymphadenopathy
  • Advanced stages
    • Dysarthria, dysphagia, odynophagia
    • Nerve involvement: ipsilateral tongue deviation (hypoglossal nerve), numbness in the chin, lower lip, and/or mandibular teeth (inferior alveolar nerve), altered tongue sensation (lingual nerve)
  • Exposure to carcinogens (e.g., alcohol, tobacco, HPV)
  • Surgical excision
  • Radiation therapy
Oral lichen planus
  • Thin, gray-white lines that form a reticular pattern (Wickham striae)
  • Painful, atrophic, and erosive lesions may be present.
  • Not fully understood
  • Associated with dental materials, drugs (e.g., NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors), infection, autoimmunity (e.g., PBC), and IBD [1]
  • High potency topical corticosteroids PLUS antifungal therapy
Oral candidiasis/oral thrush
  • White lesions that can be scraped off, resulting in red, inflamed, or bleeding areas
  • Candida species
  • Topical antifungal agent (e.g., clotrimazole, nystatin)
Atrophic glossitis
  • Painful, erythematous, edematous tongue with a smooth appearance
  • Nutritional deficiencies (e.g., iron deficiency, vitamin B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, riboflavin deficiency, niacin deficiency)
  • Infection (e.g., syphilis, candida)
  • Amyloidosis
  • Celiac disease
  • Protein-calorie malnutrition
  • Sjogren syndrome
  • Treatment of the underlying condition
Erythroplakia [2]
  • A red patch on the tongue
  • Not fully understood
  • Associated with tobacco and alcohol use
  • Tobacco and alcohol use cessation
  • Carbon dioxide laser excision
Oral hairy leukoplakia
  • Benign, painless, irregularly shaped white plaques with feathery or hairy appearance
  • Most commonly located on the lateral tongue, but can also occur on the floor of the mouth, palate, or buccal mucosa
  • Patches cannot be scraped off (in contrast to oral thrush)
  • EBV
  • Immunosuppression (e.g., patients with a history of organ transplantation)
  • Immunocompromise (e.g., HIV-positive individuals)
  • Treatment of the underlying condition
Herpetic gingivostomatitis
  • Erythema, painful erosions, and intact vesicles on perioral skin and oral mucosa, especially inner cheek, soft palate, and tongue
  • Accompanied by pharyngitis and cervical lymphadenopathy
  • HSV-1
  • Predominantly affects children
  • Treatment of HSV-1 infection (oral acyclovir) [3]
Lingual thyroid [4]
  • An abnormal mass of ectopic, thyroid tissue at the base of the tongue
  • Dysphagia for solids, dysphonia, hypothyroidism (in individuals with hypoactive thyroid tissue)
  • Occasional bleeding, upper airway obstruction
  • Failure in descent of the thyroid gland to its normal position during embryogenesis
  • Supplemental thyroxine: for symptomatic, hypothyroid patients
  • Surgical excision of the mass
  • Ablative radioiodine therapy

Tongue findings

Common tongue findings
Finding Features Associated conditions and factors Treatment
Geographic tongue (benign migratory glossitis)
  • Solitary, irregular patches of red and white tongue discoloration that resemble a geographic map
  • Lesions migrate and patients experience exacerbations and remissions over time
  • Caused by loss of filiform lingual papillae
  • Atopy
  • Psoriasis
  • Reactive arthritis
  • Topical steroid gels, topical anesthetic agents, or antihistamine mouth rinses may reduce pain or discomfort in patients with increased sensitivity to hot or spicy food.
Median rhomboid glossitis [5][6]
  • Painless area of reddish discoloration located in the middle third of the tongue, ventral to the foramen cecum
  • Smoking
  • Use of corticosteroid inhalers
  • HIV infection
  • Candida infection
  • Topical antifungals (e.g., nystatin, fluconazole, clotrimazole) in severe cases
Fissured tongue [2]
  • One or more deep grooves located on the dorsum of the tongue
  • Possibly halitosis and discoloration if food gets trapped in the fissures and causes inflammation
  • Associated with Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome
  • Psoriasis
  • Acromegaly
  • Down syndrome
  • In case debris causes inflammation, it may be necessary to rinse or brush the affected area.
Black hairy tongue [2][7]
  • Brown, black, or yellowish discoloration of the tongue with fuzzy appearance
  • Smoking
  • Coffee/tea consumption
  • Poor oral hygiene
  • Penicillin and tetracycline use
  • Seen in patients on a gastrostomy tube
  • Brushing affected area with toothbrush
  • Practicing good oral hygiene
  • Avoiding alcohol and tobacco
White tongue
  • Temporary white or gray accumulation of debris on the dorsum of the tongue
  • Accumulation of dead cells, food residue, and bacteria, promoted by:
    • Poor oral hygiene
    • Dehydration
    • Mouth breathing
    • Smoking, alcohol consumption
  • Practicing good oral hygiene
  • Avoiding alcohol and tobacco
Strawberry tongue
  • Painful, bright red swollen tongue with papillary hyperplasia resembling a strawberry/raspberry
  • Scarlet fever
  • Treatment of scarlet fever
    • Oral penicillin V or amoxicillin
    • In the case of penicillin allergy, cephalosporins, macrolides, or clindamycin can be used.
  • Kawasaki syndrome
  • Treatment of Kawasaki syndrome
    • IVIG, high-dose
    • Oral aspirin
    • IV glucocorticoids
Tongue fasciculations
  • Involuntary twitching of the tongue
  • Lower motor neuron lesion (e.g., amyotrophic lateral sclerosis)
  • Treatment of the underlying condition
Telangiectasias
  • Dilated small superficial blood vessels on the tongue
  • Osler-Weber-Rendu syndrome
  • No specific treatment for the tongue abnormality
Bluish-black patches
  • Bluish-black discoloration of the tongue and mucous membranes
  • Adrenal insufficiency
  • Treatment of the underlying condition
Macroglossia
  • Enlarged tongue with scalloped lateral margins
  • May result in cosmetic and/or functional impairment
    • Dyspnea and obstructive sleep apnea
    • Dysphagia
    • Dysphonia
    • Sialorrhea
  • Congenital disorders
    • Trisomy 21
    • Muscular hypertrophy (e.g., Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome, hemihyperplasia)
    • Vascular malformations (e.g., lymphangioma or hemangioma)
    • Multiple endocrine neoplasia type 2B
    • Glycogen storage disease type 2
    • Neurofibromatosis
  • Acquired conditions
    • Myxedema and hypothyroid disorders
    • Amyloidosis
    • Acromegaly
    • Angioedema (e.g., due to ACE inhibitors)
    • Tumors (e.g., squamous cell carcinoma of the tongue)
  • Mild cases: speech therapy
  • Severe cases: surgery (reduction glossectomy)
  • Treatment of the underlying acquired cause (e.g., radiotherapy for tumors)
Squamous cell papilloma
  • Solitary exophytic lesions with a verrucous surface
  • Human papillomavirus (mostly HPV-6 and HPV-11)
  • Surgical excision
  • Laser ablation
